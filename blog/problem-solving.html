On Problem Solving

Something I got blocked on

Recently I got blocked on something that turned out to be a really simple issue. A couple of posts ago, we talked about Flow Control - it turns out there are some really important reasons for running things in order. For example, you can't run a JavaScript function on an element that doesn't exist yet!

I tried a whole lot of things. The code itself seemed to be OK. Running the functions in the console worked. Googling the errors didn't help too much. I even had a facilitator check the code and it looked good to them!

It wasn't until I got fed up and went for a sulky walk that my brain wandered of its own accord and I realised what was happening. I'd been trying to attach an event listener to a DOM element before that element had even been created. I moved the <script> element down from the top of the page to the bottom and it worked straight away! Sometimes Usually your subconscious is smarter than you.

A problem solved in an elegant way

The most elegant way to solve problems is usually by deleting code. I recently had a problem where I'd massively overthought the issue and written far more (messy!) code than I needed to. It worked, but it would have been nearly impossible for someone to come in and make sense of what I'd done.

I realised this, so I took a step back and started doing some research on JS Methods. The problem that I was solving was fairly straightforward so I figured that it must have a simple solution. It turns out I was able to remove about 80% of my spaghetti-code and replace it with two methods and some well-defined variables.

I learned that taking the time to understand the facilities available to us in the language or framework can make your life a whole lot easier than just diving in and writing code!

Problem solving techniques

I haven't spent a lot of time or energy on pseudocode yet - I definitely understand the appeal, but it's not something that jumps to the front of my mind just yet when writing code.

AKA FAFO - This is something that I use a lot. It's definitely not the most efficient method for problem solving, but sometimes any action feels better than thinking too much about how to solve the problem.

I love the Rubber Ducky Method. I even have a "rubber ducky" on my desk at home. I've solved a lot of self-created problems by explaining, step-by-step, how I'm attempting to solve the issue at hand.

Error messages are great at finding the specific problem that's stopped things in that moment. They can often obscure the root cause, however.
